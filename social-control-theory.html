Terms

Social control theory
Travis Hirschi's idea that the pull to break the rules is in everyone, so it never really needs explaining. What needs explaining is why most people do not. His answer was social bonds.
Social bond
The ties to other people, and to ordinary life, that a person would not want to lose. Hirschi named four of them.
Attachment
Caring what the people close to you think, and not wanting to disappoint or shame them.
Commitment
Having a stake in the future, a reputation or a plan, that breaking the rules would put at risk.
Involvement
Being busy with the ordinary business of life, so there is no empty room for trouble to grow in.
Belief
Accepting that the rules are fair and worth following, even when no one is watching.
Deviance
Behaviour that breaks a social norm. Control theory treats the urge as normal and asks only what holds it back.
Value consensus
The assumption that a society shares one agreed set of rules. This activity questions whether that holds for everyone equally.
Moral panic
A wave of public alarm that casts a group as a threat, often far out of proportion to anything they have actually done.

The Theory

Social control theory, like functionalism, gives a structural explanation for why deviance happens, and builds on functionalism. It argues that deviance occurs when restraint and social control are lacking. A person becomes disengaged, which raises the potential for them to take part in deviant acts. Behaviour can be regulated through positive and negative sanctions, that is rules and laws, and through effective socialisation. Discipline from parents and the enforcement of school rules are ways that norms are set out and social control of those norms is supported.

Two forms of control

Inner
A person's interpersonal relationships: family, friends, peers, and so on.
Outer
A person's environment: their community and political or legal structures, such as courts, fines, rules, CCTV and mobile phones.

Essentially, social control theory looks at the things that bind people to society, and the influence those bonds have on behaviour. The stronger the bond, built through investment in personal relationships and environment, the less likely a person is to want to act deviantly. It is this investment in the bonds forged that keeps a person committed to conforming to the norms of their inner and outer spheres.
